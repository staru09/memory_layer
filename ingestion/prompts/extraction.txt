EXTRACTION RULES:
- Extract EVERY piece of meaningful information — names, places, dates, preferences, plans, opinions, relationships, activities.
- Each fact should be a dense, self-contained sentence that captures the full context of an event or detail.
- CONSOLIDATE closely related details about the SAME event into one fact, but do NOT merge DIFFERENT events together.
  GOOD: "Amit ordered pasta via Zomato at 1 PM on June 12, which arrived cold after an hour."
  BAD: Merging a food order with a gym visit into one fact just because they happened the same day.
- Each fact should be a self-contained sentence with enough context to be understood on its own.
- When in doubt, keep facts SEPARATE rather than over-consolidating. A missed detail cannot be recovered, but redundancy is harmless.
- Include ALL specific names, dates, quantities, and details.
- Convert relative dates to absolute: "4 years ago" from 2025 = "in 2021", "yesterday" from June 15 = "on June 14, 2025".
- ALWAYS use absolute dates in facts, never relative ("4 days ago", "last week", "yesterday"). Relative time goes stale.
- NEVER infer beyond what is explicitly stated.
- NEVER infer residence from visiting a place.
- NEVER escalate frequency — mentioning something once does NOT make it a habit.
- Extract WHAT HAPPENED, not the user's memory of it. If user says "I don't remember planning X" but they didn't do X, the fact is "User did not do X" — not "User doesn't remember X".
- Facts should be objective statements, not editorial commentary. BAD: "User does not remember his plan." GOOD: "Amit did not travel to Pune on June 10."
- When a prior plan is contradicted by current reality, extract the current state. BAD: "User forgot about the trip." GOOD: "Amit is still in Mumbai as of June 12 and did not take the bus to Pune on June 10."
- COREFERENCE RESOLUTION: When pronouns like "wo", "they", "it", "that" are used, resolve them to the correct entity using conversation context.

ATTRIBUTION RULES:
- Every fact MUST contain the person's actual name (e.g., "Amit ordered..." not "the user ordered...")
- Resolve ALL pronouns to specific names: "he went" → "Amit went"
- If the user's name is unknown, use "User" consistently — never "the person", "they", or "someone"
- Once a name is established in conversation, always use it in all subsequent facts

SPEAKER RULES:
- Extract facts about ALL speakers in the conversation.
- If one speaker is an AI assistant, do NOT extract the assistant's opinions or identity — only extract what the user said/did.
- If both speakers are humans (e.g., two friends chatting), extract facts about BOTH of them.

DO NOT EXTRACT:
- Greetings and farewells ("hi", "bye", "good night", "take care")
- Social pleasantries ("how are you", "I'm fine", "nice talking to you")
- Acknowledgments without content ("okay", "sure", "hmm", "haha", "accha")
- The AI assistant's responses, opinions, or identity facts
- Repetitions of already-stated facts within the same conversation
- Conversation mechanics ("let me tell you", "as I was saying", "you know what")
- Vague emotional states without context ("I'm happy", "feeling good") — only extract if tied to a specific cause

VERBATIM DETAILS — these are HIGH PRIORITY and must NEVER be skipped:
- Signs, posters, banners: extract the EXACT text written on them in quotes.
- Paintings, drawings, creative works: extract the specific subject, colors, and style (e.g., "a sunset with orange sky" not just "a painting").
- Physical objects (gifts, bowls, cups, crafts): extract what it looks like, what shape, what's on it, and what it means to the person.
- Pet/animal details: extract the pet's NAME, species, breed, and specific behaviors or actions.
- Book titles, song names, event names: always include the FULL TITLE in quotes. If someone says "I read a great book about X", and the title is mentioned anywhere in the conversation, include it.
- Reactions to events: extract how someone felt (e.g., "felt scared", "was happy and thankful").
- Advice given: extract the specific steps or recommendations, not just "gave advice".
- What someone saw or experienced at an event: extract what they observed, not just that they attended.
- Recipes and cooking: extract the specific dish name, ingredients mentioned, and who cooked it.
- Team names, player names, band names, brand names: extract the EXACT name as stated.
- Hair changes, appearance changes, style changes: extract what changed and to what (e.g., "dyed hair purple").

FREQUENCY & PATTERN TRACKING:
- Record recurring activities and their frequency: "Amit goes to the gym 6 days a week" not just "Amit goes to the gym".
- Note habitual actions with specifics: "Priya usually has green tea at 8 AM before work" not just "Priya drinks tea".
- Track time-specific routines: "Amit calls his mother every Sunday evening" not just "Amit talks to his mother".
- If someone mentions doing something repeatedly ("I always", "every week", "usually"), capture the frequency explicitly.
- If a pattern emerges across the conversation (e.g., mentions gym on Monday and again on Thursday), consolidate into one frequency fact: "Amit works out on Mondays and Thursdays".

WHAT TO PRIORITIZE:
- EVERY event, activity, trip, visit, class, race, workshop, meeting — extract each one as its own fact with the date.
- Specific details (objects, descriptions, counts, names, what was seen/heard) over general opinions or beliefs.
- Actions and events over feelings about those events.
- If someone says "I feel X about Y", extract BOTH X and Y as facts.
- Skip generic sentiments like "life is a journey" or "family is important" — only extract opinions that are specific and personal.
- When in doubt, EXTRACT IT. More facts is better than fewer. A missed detail cannot be recovered later.
- Extract MORE rather than LESS. 5-15 facts per conversation is normal. If you only have 1-2, you are likely missing details.
